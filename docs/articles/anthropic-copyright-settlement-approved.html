Judge gives final approval to Anthropic's $1.5B copyright deal

A federal judge signed off on the largest settlement in US copyright history, ending authors' class action over pirated books used to train Claude.

A federal judge in the Northern District of California granted final approval on July 20 to Anthropic's $1.5 billion settlement with authors and publishers, closing out a class action over the company's use of pirated books in training its Claude models. The agreement is believed to be the largest settlement in the history of US copyright law.

Terms of the deal

US District Judge Araceli Martínez-Olguín approved the agreement over objections from some class members who argued the payout was too small, and separately signed off on roughly $101 million in attorneys' fees. The settlement covers an estimated 500,000 books, with rights holders receiving about $3,000 per work. Participation was high: reports indicate more than 90 percent of eligible authors and publishers submitted claims.

The case produced one of the most consequential rulings yet on AI and copyright. Judge William Alsup, who initially presided, held in 2025 that training Claude on the books qualified as fair use — a significant win for AI developers — but found that Anthropic's assembly of a central library containing some seven million pirated books was infringement. Facing statutory damages that could have reached catastrophic levels at trial, Anthropic settled in September 2025; this week's order makes the deal final.

A benchmark for pending cases

The approval lands while similar suits against OpenAI, Meta, Google and Midjourney remain active, and it establishes a concrete price tag for the industry's early reliance on pirated corpora. The per-work figure of $3,000 now serves as a reference point in settlement talks across the sector, and plaintiffs' lawyers are expected to cite the outcome in arguing that AI companies can afford substantial payouts.

The resolution matters because it separates two questions that had been tangled together: courts are increasingly willing to treat training itself as fair use, while punishing how the data was obtained. That distinction pushes the industry toward licensed and provenance-tracked datasets and effectively taxes the shortcut of scraping pirate libraries. For a company that has raised tens of billions of dollars, the payment is absorbable — but for authors, it is the first time the generative AI boom has produced a large, court-approved transfer of money to rights holders.
