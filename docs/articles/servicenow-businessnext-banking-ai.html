ServiceNow buys $40M stake in Indian banking AI firm BusinessNext

The workflow giant paid $40M for roughly 5% of profitable Noida-based BusinessNext, valuing the banking-software maker at $700M in a joint AI push.

ServiceNow has invested $40 million in BusinessNext, a Noida-based maker of AI-powered banking software, taking a stake of roughly 5% at a $700 million valuation, TechCrunch reported. The deal nearly quadruples the Indian company's $181 million valuation from its 2021 round and anchors a go-to-market partnership aimed at financial institutions worldwide.

A profitable specialist, not a startup bet

BusinessNext is a 24-year-old, profitable firm with about $32 million in revenue in its latest fiscal year and more than $60 million in prior external funding. Its customer-relationship and lending platforms run at over 70 banks across India, Southeast Asia, the Middle East and the US — a roster that includes the Reserve Bank of India, State Bank of India and HDFC Bank. Chief executive Nishant Singh described the deal as a strategic partnership cemented with funding rather than a financial round.

The logic is complementary coverage: BusinessNext owns customer-facing banking workflows — onboarding, lending journeys, relationship management — while ServiceNow supplies back-office automation. The two plan to sell jointly to banks, with BusinessNext riding ServiceNow's global sales machine into markets where it has no presence. ServiceNow's India head Kulmeet Bawa framed the moment as Indian financial institutions moving from digital experimentation to full-scale AI-led operations.

Why it matters: the deal is a marker of where enterprise AI money is flowing — not into general-purpose copilots but into domain specialists with regulated-industry depth and existing bank relationships. For ServiceNow, which has been assembling AI capabilities across its platform, a 5% stake buys distribution rights into one of the world's fastest-growing banking markets at modest cost. And for India's software sector, a homegrown, bootstrapped-era firm commanding a $700 million valuation on AI positioning signals that the country's enterprise vendors are becoming acquisition and alliance targets, not just outsourcing partners.
