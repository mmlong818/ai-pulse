CuspAI raises $450 million to scale AI-driven materials search

The Cambridge-based startup hit a $2.6 billion valuation and unveiled a 45-member AI Materials Foundry with Nvidia, Meta and Samsung as partners.

CuspAI, a Cambridge, England-based startup applying machine learning to the discovery of new materials, announced a $450 million Series B on July 20 that values the company at $2.6 billion — roughly five times the $520 million valuation it carried after its previous round last September.

The round

Kleiner Perkins and NEA led the financing, with participation from Jeff Bezos' investment vehicle Bezos Expeditions, Lux Capital, Glade Brook Capital Partners, AMD Ventures and the UK government's Sovereign AI Venture Fund. CuspAI uses AI models to simulate how candidate materials will perform before anything is synthesized, collapsing an effectively unbounded search space into a short list worth testing in a laboratory.

An industrial coalition

Alongside the round, the company unveiled the AI Materials Foundry, a coalition of more than 45 technology and industrial organizations — among them Nvidia, Meta, Samsung, Hyundai Motor Group and Lam Research — that will pool computing resources, laboratory capacity and scientific expertise around CuspAI's platform. Early work with Nvidia will focus on hunting for materials relevant to chipmaking, an area where incremental advances in substrates, interconnects and thermal management translate directly into semiconductor performance.

Why it matters

The round marks two shifts in the current AI investment cycle. First, capital is moving beyond chatbots and coding assistants toward AI for science — companies whose value depends on producing physical-world results, such as a material that ships in a real product. Second, it is a meaningful data point for Europe: a UK startup drawing top-tier Silicon Valley firms, Bezos money and direct state backing suggests the region can still anchor globally competitive AI companies in deep-tech niches, even as the largest foundation-model players remain American and Chinese. The consortium structure is worth watching too — if the Foundry model works, it offers a template for how capital-intensive scientific AI can share compute and laboratory costs that no single startup can carry alone.
