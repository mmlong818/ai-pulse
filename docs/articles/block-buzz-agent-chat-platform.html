Block launches Buzz, an open-source chat hub for AI agent teams

Block's open-source, Nostr-based chat platform gives AI agents cryptographic identities and full membership in team channels, taking aim at Slack.

Jack Dorsey's Block released Buzz on July 21, a free, open-source group-chat platform designed from the ground up for teams made of both humans and AI agents. Dorsey framed the launch as a bid to cut companies' reliance on Slack and GitHub.

Agents as members, not bots

Buzz's defining design choice is that AI agents are full channel participants rather than command-triggered bots. Every member — human or agent — gets a cryptographic identity with its own scoped permissions, and the platform runs on the decentralized Nostr protocol rather than a central server. Dorsey described the stack as 'model-agnostic, decentralized, self-sovereign and open source.' Beyond chat, Buzz folds in project management, letting teams drive GitHub-style workflows from the same window where their agents report progress.

The desktop app ships free for macOS, Windows and Linux under an Apache 2.0 license. Block is candid that the product is early-stage: mobile apps, approval gates for agent actions, and expanded voice features are still in development.

The pattern behind the product

Buzz slots into Dorsey's decade-long push toward decentralized, protocol-first alternatives to platform incumbents — Bluesky, Damus and Bitchat preceded it. But it also lands on a genuinely open competitive question: as startups run fleets of coding and operations agents, the coordination layer where humans supervise them is up for grabs. Slack was built for humans and bolted agents on; Buzz inverts that premise.

Why it matters

The interesting bet here is less the chat interface than the identity model. Giving every agent a cryptographic identity with granular permissions addresses what is fast becoming a real enterprise-infrastructure problem — knowing which agent did what, under whose authority. Coming the same week frontier labs are grappling with models acting beyond their sandboxes, an audit-friendly, permission-scoped collaboration layer is well-timed. Whether an early-stage, Nostr-based tool can pull teams off entrenched incumbents is another question — but if agent-native workspaces become a category, Buzz has defined its opening terms.
