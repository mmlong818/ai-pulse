Sony sues Udio over 30,117 songs, exposure balloons to $4.5B

A new SDNY complaint says discovery surfaced 30,117 Sony recordings in Udio's training data, pushing potential damages from $50 million toward $4.5 billion.

Sony Music Entertainment filed a fresh copyright lawsuit against AI music generator Udio in the Southern District of New York on July 20, alleging that discovery in its ongoing case revealed 30,117 of its sound recordings inside Udio's training datasets.

Why a second lawsuit

Sony first sued Udio (alongside rival Suno) in June 2024, together with Universal Music Group and Warner Music Group, over a few hundred named recordings. When discovery surfaced the far larger set, Sony moved to fold those tracks into the existing case — but Judge Alvin K. Hellerstein denied the amendment last month. Rather than let the claims lapse, Sony refiled them as a standalone complaint, a procedural workaround that keeps every recording in play.

The new suit raises three claims: infringement of post-1972 recordings, infringement of pre-1972 recordings protected under the Music Modernization Act, and circumvention of technological protection measures under the DMCA. Its practical effect is arithmetic: expanding the recordings at issue from 333 to more than 30,000 balloons Udio's theoretical statutory-damages exposure from roughly $50 million to about $4.5 billion. Sony's complaint argues that copying protected recordings to build a commercial AI product 'requires permission from rightsholders' — full stop.

Why it matters

A $4.5 billion exposure figure is existential for a startup of Udio's size, and it arrives days after a judge granted final approval to Anthropic's $1.5 billion settlement with book authors — a deal that established a going rate for training-data claims and, at roughly $3,000 per work, makes the per-song math against Udio look brutal. The case also shows how discovery is reshaping AI copyright litigation: plaintiffs no longer need to infer training-set contents from model outputs when court-ordered disclosure can enumerate them. For every generative-media company that trained on unlicensed catalogs, the message is that the true size of the liability may only emerge once the documents do.
