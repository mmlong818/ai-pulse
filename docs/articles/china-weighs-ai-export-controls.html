China weighs export curbs on AI models, chips and training data

Beijing is consulting Alibaba, ByteDance and Zhipu AI on restricting exports of model weights, training data and chip designs, per an FT report.

China's Commerce Ministry is considering export controls covering advanced AI models, chip designs and training data, according to a Financial Times report published this week. The ministry has consulted domestic AI leaders including Alibaba, ByteDance and Zhipu AI on measures that could limit the transfer of key training data overseas and restrict foreign users from downloading the weights of Chinese-developed models.

What the measures would cover

The proposals under discussion extend beyond models. Regulators have also sought industry views on rules that would bar overseas chipmakers, including TSMC and Qualcomm, from manufacturing advanced semiconductors based on designs from Chinese companies such as Huawei, Alibaba and ByteDance. Any final measures could be folded into the next revision of China's catalogue of technologies prohibited or restricted from export. Officials are still weighing feedback, and no decision has been announced.

The move would mark a striking reversal. Chinese labs have spent the past two years building global influence precisely by releasing capable, inexpensive open-weight models — Alibaba's Qwen family, DeepSeek's releases and Zhipu's GLM line among them — that developers worldwide can download, modify and build on. That strategy has won meaningful adoption inside Western companies and positioned Chinese systems as alternatives to closed US models. Restricting weight downloads would not cut off access entirely, since foreign customers could still reach the models through cloud APIs, but it would end the ability to run and adapt them directly.

A mirror of US policy

The deliberations echo the export-control playbook Washington has applied to China since 2022, but in reverse: Beijing now sees its own AI stack as strategic technology worth guarding. The timing is notable, coming days before planned US-China talks that are expected to touch on open-weight model releases and military AI.

This matters because open-weight Chinese models have become core infrastructure for startups and researchers far beyond China, and any curb on their distribution would ripple through the global open-source ecosystem. It would also signal that both superpowers now treat frontier AI as a controlled export, accelerating the bifurcation of the technology into separate spheres — with developers in third countries facing harder choices about which stack to build on.
