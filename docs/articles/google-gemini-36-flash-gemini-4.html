Google ships Gemini 3.6 Flash as Gemini 4 pretraining begins

Google released three Flash-tier Gemini models and said Gemini 4 is in pretraining, while the delayed Gemini 3.5 Pro remains unshipped.

Google released three new models in its Gemini family on July 21: Gemini 3.6 Flash, Gemini 3.5 Flash-Lite, and a security-focused variant called Gemini 3.5 Flash Cyber. Alongside the launch, the company confirmed it has started what it calls its most ambitious pretraining run to date for Gemini 4, its next-generation flagship, though it gave no release timeline.

A cheaper, leaner workhorse

Gemini 3.6 Flash is positioned as the everyday model for high-volume workloads, priced at $1.50 per million input tokens and $7.50 per million output tokens. Google says it improves coding and multimodal performance while producing roughly 17 percent fewer output tokens than its predecessor, a meaningful efficiency gain for developers who pay by the token. Its knowledge cutoff has been extended to March 2026. Flash-Lite, at $0.30 per million input tokens, targets latency-sensitive applications with throughput of around 350 output tokens per second.

The third model breaks with Google's usual distribution playbook. Flash Cyber, tuned for vulnerability discovery and other security tasks, is being withheld from general API access and offered only to governments and vetted partners through a pilot program. That mirrors a broader industry pattern of gating offense-capable security models, seen in Anthropic's and Microsoft's restricted offerings.

The missing flagship

The releases arrive against an awkward backdrop: Gemini 3.5 Pro, which Google had signaled for June, is still confined to partner testing, reportedly held back by coding-performance issues. That leaves Google competing at the frontier with an aging flagship while rivals ship new top-end models. Confirming the Gemini 4 pretraining run appears designed to reassure developers and enterprises that a next-generation answer is coming.

The announcement matters because it crystallizes a two-track strategy now common across major labs: compete aggressively on price and efficiency in the mid-tier, where most production workloads live, while betting the frontier race on the next big training run. Falling Flash-tier prices intensify the ongoing inference price war, which benefits developers in the short term. But Google's willingness to skip a delayed flagship generation and lean on Gemini 4 also shows how much the industry's center of gravity has shifted to ever-larger pretraining bets, with each cycle raising the stakes in compute and capital.
