US and China to hold first formal AI talks in September

Washington and Beijing plan their first dedicated talks on frontier AI risks in September, ahead of Xi Jinping's planned visit to the United States.

The United States and China plan to hold formal government-to-government talks on artificial intelligence in September, Reuters reported on July 21, citing people familiar with the preparations. The dialogue would be led on the American side by Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and is expected to convene before Chinese President Xi Jinping's planned September 24 visit to the United States, though dates have not been finalised.

From summit pledge to negotiating table

The channel grows out of the May 2026 summit between President Donald Trump and Xi. Bessent signalled at the time that follow-up meetings would aim at halting the proliferation of powerful AI models to non-state actors. The September agenda is expected to run broader, covering military applications of AI, cyberattack risks, biosecurity, access to advanced models and the treatment of open-weight systems that can be freely downloaded and run anywhere.

Both capitals arrive having recently hardened their positions. Washington's June executive order created a classified benchmarking process to identify frontier models with advanced cyber capabilities, paired with a voluntary pre-release review window negotiated with leading US labs. Beijing has meanwhile been consulting domestic champions including Alibaba, ByteDance and Zhipu AI on possible export controls covering advanced models, chips and training data. According to Asia-based reporting, Chinese negotiators want clarity on how Washington intends to govern future frontier-model releases and whether restrictions on Chinese open-weight models are under consideration.

Why it matters

This would be the first standing bilateral mechanism on AI between the two countries that together account for the overwhelming majority of frontier-model development. Until now the relationship has been managed almost entirely through unilateral tools — export controls, entity lists and investment restrictions — with no forum for discussing risks neither side can contain alone, from model misuse by criminal groups to military escalation. Whether the talks yield substantive guardrails or serve mainly as staging for Xi's visit, the creation of a formal channel marks a structural shift: AI has joined trade and nuclear arms on the short list of issues the two powers treat as requiring direct, continuous negotiation.
