TSMC in talks to raise chipmaking prices up to 10% from 2027

Nikkei reports TSMC is negotiating rises of 5-10% on advanced and mature nodes from 2027, with heavy AI orders facing surcharges of up to 15% more.

Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co. is negotiating with customers to raise foundry prices by up to 10 percent starting in 2027, Nikkei Asia reported on July 21. The talks reportedly began in June and cover both advanced and mature process nodes.

A two-tier increase

According to the report, baseline increases for advanced processes at 7 nanometers and below will run between 5 and 10 percent, varying by customer and product, with mature nodes also affected. On top of that, high-performance computing orders that exceed customers' original forecasts — in practice, the AI accelerator volumes straining TSMC's fabs — could carry an additional premium of 10 to 15 percent, pushing effective increases well past 10 percent for some AI-heavy clients. Tom's Hardware calculates that worst-case combinations could approach 25 percent. TSMC has reportedly chosen to delay implementation until 2027 to give customers time to adjust.

Why raise prices now

The company is contending with rising costs for materials and manufacturing equipment alongside the expense of building fabs outside Taiwan, in the United States, Japan and Germany, where construction and operating costs run far higher. At the same time, AI demand has kept its leading-edge capacity effectively sold out, giving the foundry unusual pricing power.

The move matters because TSMC manufactures nearly all of the world's leading-edge AI silicon — Nvidia and AMD accelerators, Apple's processors, and the custom chips designed by hyperscalers. A foundry price increase propagates directly through GPU prices into cloud compute and, ultimately, the cost of AI tokens. It also carries an implicit forecast: by scheduling premiums specifically on above-forecast HPC orders, TSMC is signaling it expects AI demand to outrun supply well into 2027. For an industry banking on inference costs falling forever, the message from the bottom of the stack is that the compute cost floor is quietly rising.
