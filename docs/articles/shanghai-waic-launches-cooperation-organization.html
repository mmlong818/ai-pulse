Shanghai AI conference closes with 29-nation cooperation body

The Shanghai conference ended July 20 after China launched a 29-nation World AI Cooperation Organization pitched as a more equitable governance model.

The 2026 World Artificial Intelligence Conference closed in Shanghai on July 20 after a four-day run that featured Xi Jinping's first keynote address at the event and the formal launch of the World Artificial Intelligence Cooperation Organization (WAICO), a new intergovernmental body headquartered in Shanghai with 29 founding member states.

A China-led governance body

WAICO grew out of a proposal Beijing floated at last year's conference. Founding members who signed the agreement include Russia, Indonesia, Pakistan, Kazakhstan and Laos, alongside a broader group drawn largely from the developing world. The organization's stated mission is to promote fair and equitable AI governance and to help close the technology gap between rich and poor countries, including access to models, computing power and talent. In his address, Xi urged that AI be treated as an international public good rather than a technology monopolized by a handful of countries and companies.

Notably absent from the founding roster are the United States, European Union member states and other G7 economies, which have organized their own governance tracks through the OECD, the G7 Hiroshima process and a series of AI safety summits.

Record-scale showcase

The conference doubled as an industrial exhibition of unprecedented size: organizers reported more than 1,100 exhibiting companies across over 100,000 square meters of floor space and upward of 3,000 products on display, spanning large language models, AI agents and open-source industrial ecosystems. Several Chinese labs timed announcements to the event, including Alibaba's preview of its 2.4-trillion-parameter Qwen3.8-Max model.

Why it matters

Global AI governance is consolidating into rival poles. WAICO gives China an institutional vehicle for exporting its preferred norms — sovereignty-centric and development-focused — to a bloc of countries that feel excluded from Western-led processes, much as its infrastructure diplomacy did in earlier technology cycles. Whether the body produces binding standards or remains largely symbolic, its existence makes a single global framework for frontier-AI rules considerably less likely, and it hands developing countries new leverage to play competing blocs against each other.
