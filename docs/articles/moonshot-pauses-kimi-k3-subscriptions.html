Moonshot halts new Kimi K3 sign-ups as demand outstrips GPUs

Moonshot AI froze new Kimi K3 subscriptions two days after launch as demand saturated its GPUs, days before a planned July 27 open-weights release.

Moonshot AI stopped accepting new subscriptions for Kimi K3 on July 19, roughly 48 hours after the model's launch, saying user demand had pushed its GPU clusters to their limits. "Kimi K3 has received far more love than we expected, and our GPUs are feeling it," the Beijing-based startup wrote on X, adding that existing subscribers are unaffected and that new slots will reopen in batches as capacity comes online.

A 3-trillion-class open model

Kimi K3 is a roughly 2.8-trillion-parameter system with native vision capabilities and a one-million-token context window. Moonshot bills it as the first open model in the three-trillion-parameter class, aimed at long-horizon coding, knowledge work and reasoning. Early leaderboard results placed it at or near the top of coding rankings, which helped drive the surge of sign-ups that overwhelmed the company's infrastructure.

The company still plans to publish the model's weights on July 27. If that happens on schedule, K3 would become the largest open-weight frontier model released to date.

Compute as the binding constraint

The episode illustrates the squeeze facing Chinese AI firms: demand for their strongest models now outruns the serving capacity available under US chip export restrictions. Moonshot's open-weight strategy is partly a response — publishing the weights lets cloud providers and developers around the world shoulder inference loads that its own clusters cannot carry.

Why it matters

A frontier lab turning away paying customers within two days of launch is a vivid data point on both the quality of China's current model crop and the hard ceiling its compute supply imposes. The July 27 weights release will be closely watched: a genuinely competitive 2.8-trillion-parameter open model would reset the baseline for what is freely available, with knock-on effects for pricing at closed-model vendors — and fresh questions for export-control policy, since open weights, once published, are beyond any jurisdiction's reach.
