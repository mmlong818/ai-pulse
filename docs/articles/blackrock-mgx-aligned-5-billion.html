BlackRock-MGX group commits $5 billion to Aligned Data Centers

After closing its $40 billion acquisition of Aligned Data Centers, the BlackRock and MGX-backed consortium is injecting $5 billion to expand AI capacity.

A consortium that includes BlackRock's Global Infrastructure Partners and Abu Dhabi's MGX has committed $5 billion in growth capital to Aligned Data Centers, days after completing its roughly $40 billion acquisition of the company from Macquarie Asset Management. The purchase ranks among the largest data-centre transactions on record, and the fresh equity is earmarked for expanding capacity across Aligned's development pipeline.

Scaling a 6.4-gigawatt platform

Founded in 2013 and headquartered in Plano, Texas, Aligned builds and operates facilities for hyperscalers and large cloud customers, with 51 campuses and more than 6.4 gigawatts of operational and planned capacity across the Americas. The buyers are anchored in the AI Infrastructure Partnership, a coalition whose backers include Microsoft and Nvidia and which has said it aims to deploy $30 billion of equity — potentially reaching $100 billion including debt — to build out computing capacity for AI.

The commitment lands amid an unrelenting infrastructure spending cycle: analysts tracking the sector note that investment is increasingly flowing toward compute, power and data pipelines rather than model development itself, as hyperscalers and AI labs compete to lock in capacity years ahead of delivery.

Why it matters

The deal shows how AI data centres have hardened into an institutional asset class, drawing pension-scale and sovereign capital — notably from the Gulf, where MGX has become one of the most aggressive investors in AI infrastructure — rather than just venture money. It also underlines where the bottleneck now sits: not chips alone, but powered, permitted land and grid connections, which is precisely what a platform like Aligned sells. The wager embedded in these numbers is that AI demand keeps compounding for years; if it does, capacity locked in today is a moat, and if it does not, the sector will have built one of the most expensive overhangs in infrastructure history. Either way, decisions shaping the future cost and geography of AI compute are increasingly made by infrastructure funds, not technology companies.
