Kalanick's Atoms raises $1.7B from a16z for industrial robotics

Travis Kalanick's rebranded Atoms raised $1.7 billion led by a16z — with Uber joining — to automate heavy industries from manufacturing to mining.

Atoms, the industrial robotics and AI automation company led by Uber co-founder Travis Kalanick, announced a $1.7 billion funding round on July 22, led by Andreessen Horowitz with participation from Bain Capital, Fifth Wall — and, in a notable turn, Uber itself. The company's valuation was not disclosed.

From ghost kitchens to heavy industry

Atoms is the rebranded holding company previously known as CloudKitchens, Kalanick's post-Uber venture in delivery-only restaurant real estate. The new identity reflects a much broader ambition: applying robotics and AI automation to heavy industries including vehicles, mining, and manufacturing. Kalanick describes the vision as building "atoms-based computers" — treating manufacturing as the CPU, real estate as storage, and transportation as the network — and says the funding will go toward hiring across mechanical engineering and software architecture, including work on what the company calls a "wheelbase for robots."

Kalanick cast the round as "a bit of unfinished business," tying it to the journey he began sixteen years ago "to digitize the physical world." Ben Horowitz, whose firm led the round, endorsed the founder in characteristically direct terms, calling Kalanick "that guy" capable of transforming the old-school, heavy parts of the economy. Uber's participation adds symbolic weight, reuniting the company with the co-founder who was ousted from it in 2017.

Why it matters

At $1.7 billion, this is one of the year's largest robotics rounds, and it lands in a sector where capital is consolidating fast around physical-world AI — from humanoid startups to warehouse automation. The bet here is different from the humanoid wave: Atoms is targeting unglamorous, capital-intensive industries where labor shortages and cost pressure make automation economics straightforward. A win would validate the thesis that the next AI fortune lies in atoms rather than tokens; with a16z writing its biggest checks in that direction, the venture market is already voting.
