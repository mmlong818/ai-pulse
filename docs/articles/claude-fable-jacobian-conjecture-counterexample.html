Claude Fable 5 counterexample topples 1939 Jacobian conjecture

An Anthropic mathematician says Claude Fable 5 produced a hand-checkable counterexample to the 87-year-old Jacobian conjecture, verified within a day.

The Jacobian conjecture, posed by Ott-Heinrich Keller in 1939, asserts that any polynomial map whose Jacobian determinant is a non-zero constant must have a polynomial inverse. On July 20, Anthropic mathematician Levent Alpöge posted a short polynomial formula on X that he says was found with the company's Claude Fable 5 model — and that breaks the conjecture in three dimensions.

A counterexample anyone can check

The map, from C^3 to C^3, has a constant Jacobian determinant of -2, satisfying Keller's hypothesis exactly. Yet it sends three distinct points — (0, 0, -1/4), (1, -3/2, 13/2) and (-1, 3/2, 13/2) — to the same output, so it cannot be inverted at all, let alone polynomially. Because the formula is explicit and compact, checking it requires only routine computation. Mathematicians reproduced the verification within a day, by hand and with computer algebra systems, and blog write-ups from working algebraists concur that the construction holds. The result falsifies the conjecture in dimension three and above; the original two-variable case remains open, and no formal paper has yet appeared.

From a throwaway post to 20 million views

Alpöge announced the result with striking informality — "hello there the jacobian conjecture is false" — crediting a friend's casual question and noting the model worked on the problem during the World Cup final. The post has drawn more than 20 million views, and Fortune reported that mathematics departments spent the following day absorbed by the finding, with some researchers describing the pace of AI-driven results as unsettling.

The episode matters because a counterexample is self-certifying: nobody needs to trust the model, its training data or its reasoning traces — only to substitute numbers into a formula. Arriving weeks after Fable 5's public release, and amid a wave of contested claims about AI mathematics, it is the clearest demonstration to date that a frontier model can contribute a decisive, novel object to research mathematics, and it will sharpen the debate over how the field adapts when century-old conjectures can fall overnight.
