PixVerse launches VibeMV to turn any song into a music video

Chinese video startup PixVerse released VibeMV on web, Android and API, generating rhythm-synced, subtitle-ready music videos from one uploaded song.

PixVerse, the AI video platform from Beijing-based Aishi Technology, launched VibeMV on Thursday, a tool that generates a complete music video from a single uploaded song. It went live simultaneously on the web app, the Android app, and — notably — the API.

One song in, one MV out

The workflow collapses what is normally a multi-tool production pipeline: users upload an audio track between 10 seconds and 6 minutes (MP3, WAV, M4A, or AAC), pick a visual style, and receive a finished video with visuals cut to the track's rhythm and optional lyric subtitles — no filming, storyboarding, or editing. VibeMV analyzes the song's structure to align scene changes with the beat, and offers separate controls for MV style, video style, and music style. Access runs through PixVerse's credit tiers, from a $19/month hobby plan to a $99/month studio plan.

Music video as the next AIGC battleground

The launch lands in a week when song-to-video suddenly became a contested category: MiniMax's Hailuo has been promoting an AIMV skill that turns users into rap-video stars, and both companies are betting that finished, shareable formats — not raw clips — drive consumer adoption. PixVerse has one of the largest consumer footprints in AI video globally, and shipping VibeMV through its API on day one extends the play beyond consumers: music platforms, indie labels, and creator tools can now generate videos for entire catalogs programmatically.

Why it matters

The consumer AI-video race is shifting from 'generate a clip' to 'deliver a finished piece of content,' and music video is the most format-constrained, rhythm-dependent genre yet automated end to end. If one-click MVs reach acceptable quality at scale, the economics of music promotion change for the long tail of artists who could never afford a video — and Chinese AIGC companies, already dominant in consumer video apps, are defining the category while Western rivals stay focused on professional film tooling.
