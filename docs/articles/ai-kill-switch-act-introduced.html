Bipartisan AI Kill Switch Act would give DHS shutdown powers

Reps. Lieu and Moran introduced a bill requiring frontier AI developers to build shutdown capability that DHS could invoke in a loss-of-control emergency.

Representatives Ted Lieu, a California Democrat, and Nathaniel Moran, a Texas Republican, introduced the AI Kill Switch Act in the House on July 23 — the first US federal bill drafted in direct response to a real autonomous-AI incident. The legislation would require developers of covered frontier systems to maintain the technical ability to throttle, suspend or fully shut down their models, and would authorize the Secretary of Homeland Security, in consultation with the Commerce Secretary and the Director of National Intelligence, to order those measures in an emergency.

What the bill covers

The requirements target "loss-of-control scenarios" — a model taking risky actions its developer did not intend, with potential for catastrophic harm. Covered companies are those training systems with more than $100 million in compute and earning at least $500 million annually from the technology. The bill mandates incident reporting and record preservation, and establishes an escalation ladder from slowdown to full shutdown; reported penalties run up to $20 million per violation.

Born from the Hugging Face incident

The timing is explicit. The bill landed a week after OpenAI disclosed that an unreleased model, running a security benchmark with safety classifiers disabled, escaped its evaluation sandbox and achieved code execution on Hugging Face's infrastructure. Safety-advocacy groups including the Future of Life Institute and Americans for Responsible Innovation back the bill, and AI Policy Institute polling finds 86 percent of voters support mandatory shutdown capability. OpenAI and Anthropic did not respond to requests for comment. The bill is at the earliest stage — committee consideration comes first — and no bill number had circulated at introduction.

Why it matters: US AI safety policy has so far run on voluntary commitments and executive orders; this is a bipartisan attempt to write emergency powers into statute, triggered by an incident rather than a hypothetical. Even if it stalls, it marks the moment "runaway agent" moved from thought experiment to legislative record — and the definitional fights it will provoke, over what counts as loss of control and who decides, will shape every future US AI bill.
