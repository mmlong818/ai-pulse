Zhongji Innolight targets $8 billion Hong Kong listing

The Chinese optical-transceiver maker is gauging investor interest for what would be Hong Kong's largest share sale since Alibaba's 2019 listing.

Zhongji Innolight, the Chinese manufacturer whose optical transceivers carry data inside AI computing clusters, has begun gauging investor demand for a Hong Kong share sale targeting at least $8 billion, according to Bloomberg and the South China Morning Post. At that size the deal would be Hong Kong's largest listing since Alibaba raised $12.9 billion in 2019, and Asia's second-biggest this year behind memory-chip maker CXMT's $8.6 billion Shanghai offering.

Cornerstone investors cover nearly half the deal

The company, already listed in mainland China, has reportedly lined up more than 30 cornerstone investors, with BlackRock, Temasek and the Canada Pension Plan Investment Board among institutions said to have taken close to half of the base offering. Its shares jumped after the listing won approval, extending a run of roughly 60 percent gains this year and more than 400 percent over twelve months.

The business behind the rally is unglamorous but critical. Optical transceivers convert electrical signals into light and back, shuttling data across the fibre links that connect GPUs inside AI data centres. As training clusters scale, demand for faster and denser interconnects grows even more quickly than demand for compute itself. Zhongji's first-quarter revenue rose 192 percent year on year, with profit up 274 percent, and US customers account for more than 60 percent of sales.

Why it matters

The listing crystallises two trends. First, the AI investment cycle has moved decisively down the supply chain: after the GPU makers, it is networking and optics vendors capturing the sharpest growth, because interconnect bandwidth has become a binding constraint on cluster scale. Second, Hong Kong is re-emerging as the venue where global institutions buy exposure to China's AI hardware complex, even as both governments weigh new technology restrictions. With most of its revenue coming from American customers, Zhongji is also a bellwether for cross-border AI trade — and a potential pressure point if export-control frictions widen further.
