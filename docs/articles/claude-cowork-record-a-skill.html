Anthropic's Claude Cowork turns screen recordings into skills

A new 'Record a skill' feature lets paid subscribers teach Claude desktop workflows by recording their screen and narrating the steps aloud.

Anthropic on July 21 rolled out 'Record a skill', a Claude Cowork feature that lets users teach the assistant desktop workflows by demonstration rather than description. A user starts a capture from the '+' menu in Cowork — the workspace section of the Claude desktop app — performs a task on screen while explaining the steps aloud, then stops recording. Claude converts the screen activity, clicks, keystrokes and voice narration into a structured, reusable skill it can replay when the same task comes up again.

Demonstration instead of prompt engineering

The feature ships to paying subscribers on Pro, Max and Team plans. Anthropic is pitching it as a way to automate repetitive digital chores — filing expenses, formatting reports, triaging inboxes — without users having to articulate their process in carefully engineered prompts or code. The approach acknowledges a persistent gap in AI assistants: many workflows are easier to show than to describe, and much of the knowledge that makes them work is tacit. Anthropic is not alone in this bet; OpenAI offers comparable workflow-recording capabilities through its Codex tooling, and desktop automation has become an active front in the competition among assistant makers.

Why it matters

Programming by demonstration has a decades-long research history, but pairing it with models that can watch a screen, listen to reasoning and generalise the intent behind clicks makes it practical for mainstream users for the first time. If it works reliably, it moves office automation from the domain of RPA consultants and power users to anyone able to narrate their own job — which is both the promise and the tension, given what that implies for routine white-collar work. It also deepens the data relationship between users and AI vendors: screen recordings of real workflows are among the most sensitive and most valuable artifacts an employee can share, and enterprises will need policies for what gets recorded, where skills are stored and who can replay them.
